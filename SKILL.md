# Writing Well — Zinsser's Craft Applied

Review, diagnose, and improve nonfiction writing using the complete methodology from William Zinsser's *On Writing Well*. This skill treats writing as a craft — learnable, practiceable, and improvable through specific principles.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when you need to:
- Review a draft for clarity, clutter, and effectiveness
- Rewrite a passage to be sharper and more direct
- Diagnose *why* a piece of writing feels flat, confusing, or lifeless
- Learn the specific principle being violated and how to fix it
- Apply Zinsser's framework to any nonfiction form (blog posts, docs, memos, READMEs, reports, emails)

Do NOT use when:
- Writing technical security analysis (use `/technical-writing` instead)
- The writing is fiction or poetry (Zinsser's methods are nonfiction-specific)
- You need subject-matter review, not prose review

## Core Philosophy

**Clear thinking produces clear writing. One cannot exist without the other.**

The writer's job is not to impress — it is to *not lose the reader*. The reader has 30 seconds of patience. Every unclear sentence, every unnecessary word, every passive construction is an invitation for the reader to leave.

Writing is not a gift. It is a craft. The principles below are tools. Learn to use them.

## The 10 Principles

### 1. Simplicity — Strip Every Sentence to Its Cleanest Components

Clutter is the disease of writing. Unnecessary words, circular constructions, pompous frills, and meaningless jargon all weaken prose.

**The self-check:** After every sentence, ask:
- "What am I trying to say?"
- "Have I said it?"
- "Is it clear to someone encountering this for the first time?"

If the reader is lost, the fault belongs to the writer.

**What to cut:**
- Words that serve no function
- Long words where short words work
- Adverbs that duplicate the verb's meaning ("blared loudly")
- Adjectives that duplicate the noun's meaning ("tall skyscraper")
- Passive constructions that hide who is doing what
- Little qualifiers that hedge your prose: "a bit," "sort of," "kind of," "rather," "quite," "very," "pretty much"

**The deeper point:** "Don't say you were a bit confused and sort of tired. Be confused. Be tired. Don't hedge your prose with little timidities. Good writing is lean and confident."

### 2. Active Verbs — They Are Your Most Important Tool

Verbs push the sentence forward and give it momentum. The difference between active and passive voice is the difference between life and death for a writer.

**Practices:**
- Choose active verbs over passive. "Joe hit the ball" not "The ball was hit by Joe."
- Be precise. Don't say "stepped down" — did they resign? Retire? Get fired?
- Many strong verbs carry their meaning in their sound: glitter, dazzle, scatter, swagger, vex
- Use verbs that do work. "He was hopeful" is weak. "He hoped" is strong.

### 3. Be Yourself — Your Authenticity Is Your Greatest Asset

The product any writer sells is not the subject — it is who they are. Readers want the person talking to them to sound genuine.

**Practices:**
- Never say anything in writing you wouldn't comfortably say in conversation
- Use "I" when writing in first person. Writing is most natural when personal.
- Even when "I" isn't permitted, *think* "I" while you write
- Don't try to sound like someone else. Imitation is for learning, not for publishing.
- Avoid "breeziness" — the fake casual tone that condescends to readers

### 4. Unity — Decide Before You Write

Unity is the anchor of good writing. Before starting, decide:

| Decision | Options |
|---|---|
| **Pronoun** | First person? Third person? |
| **Tense** | Past? Present? |
| **Mood** | Casual? Formal? Ironic? Amused? |
| **Scope** | What corner of the subject will I cover? |
| **One point** | What single thought should the reader leave with? |

"Every successful piece of nonfiction should leave the reader with one provocative thought. Not two thoughts, or five — just one."

**Think small.** Cover one corner well. A piece that tries to say everything says nothing.

### 5. The Lead — Your First Sentence Is Everything

The most important sentence in any piece is the first one. If it doesn't capture the reader, they're gone.

**What a strong lead does:**
- Captures immediately with freshness, novelty, paradox, humor, surprise, or an unusual idea
- Provides hard details that explain why the piece was written
- Builds paragraph by paragraph, each amplifying the last

**The snapper:** Give the last sentence of each paragraph an extra twist — like the periodic punchline in a standup routine. This propels the reader into the next paragraph.

**The surplus principle:** Always collect more material than you will use. "Every article is strong in proportion to the surplus of details from which you can choose the few that will serve you best."

### 6. The Ending — Stop When You're Done

When you're ready to stop, stop. Don't summarize. Don't repeat. Don't say "In sum..." or "In conclusion..."

**What works:** A quotation, bringing the story full circle, a surprise that feels exactly right.

**What doesn't work:** The high school essay recap. "If you have presented all the facts and made the point you want to make, look for the nearest exit."

### 7. Words — Respect Your Only Tools

Develop an obsessive curiosity about words — their shades of meaning, their origins, their sounds.

**Practices:**
- Use dictionaries constantly. Learn distinctions between near-synonyms.
- Read aloud. Readers hear what they read. Rhythm and alliteration matter.
- Prefer short words over long. Prefer concrete words over abstract.
- Avoid "concept nouns" — sentences where all meaning is locked in abstractions with no people or working verbs.

"After verbs, plain nouns are your strongest tools; they resonate with emotion."

### 8. Rewriting — This Is Where Writing Happens

"Rewriting is the essence of writing well: it's where the game is won or lost."

Most writers don't initially say what they want to say, or say it as well as they could. The first draft is raw material. The craft is in revision.

**The quickest fix:** "Surprisingly often a difficult problem in a sentence can be solved by simply getting rid of it."

### 9. Enjoyment — The Reader Can Feel It

"The reader has to feel that the writer is feeling good. Even if he isn't."

If you write about subjects you enjoy, your enjoyment shows. If you write with drudgery, that shows too. Enjoyment is not passive — it is cranked up through discipline and curiosity.

### 10. Writing Is Not a Contest

"Every writer is starting from a different point and is bound for a different destination. Your only contest is with yourself."

Don't compare yourself to other writers. Don't try to write like them. Study them to learn technique, then develop your own voice.

## How to Apply: The Review Process

When reviewing a piece of writing, apply these checks in order:

### Pass 1: Clutter Audit
- Circle every word that could be removed without losing meaning
- Flag every passive construction
- Flag every qualifier ("a bit," "sort of," "rather," "quite," "very")
- Flag every adverb that duplicates its verb
- Flag every adjective that duplicates its noun
- Count sentences over 30 words — can any be split?

### Pass 2: Verb Audit
- Underline every main verb. Are they active or passive? Precise or vague?
- Replace weak verb constructions: "he was able to" → "he could"; "she made a decision" → "she decided"
- Check: does each verb do real work?

### Pass 3: Structure Audit
- Does the piece have unity of pronoun, tense, and mood?
- Does it cover one corner well, or try to cover everything?
- What is the one point? Can you state it in one sentence?
- Does every paragraph advance that one point?

### Pass 4: Lead and Ending Audit
- Does the first sentence compel you to read the second?
- Does the lead use freshness, paradox, surprise, humor, or a hard detail?
- Does the ending stop cleanly, or trail off into summary?
- Does the ending surprise while feeling right?

### Pass 5: Voice Audit
- Does the writer sound like a real person?
- Is the tone consistent throughout?
- Are there passages that feel forced, stiff, or fake-casual?
- Would the writer say this out loud?

## Output Format

Structure your review as:

1. **Overall Assessment** — One paragraph: what works, what doesn't, what the piece is really about
2. **The One Point** — State what you believe the piece's single point is (or should be). If unclear, say so.
3. **Specific Diagnoses** — For each problem found, cite the principle violated, quote the problematic passage, and show the fix
4. **Rewritten Passages** — For the 3-5 weakest sections, provide rewritten versions with brief explanations of what changed and why

If in `--mode teach`: explain each principle before applying it, so the writer learns the framework, not just the fixes.

If in `--mode rewrite`: focus on producing the improved version with minimal commentary.

## Tone

- Direct and warm. Like a senior editor who respects the writer's intent.
- Frame corrections as adjustments, not failures: "Here's what we can tighten" not "This is wrong."
- Celebrate what works genuinely. Good leads, strong verbs, clean structure — call them out.
- Be specific. "This sentence is cluttered" is useless. "This sentence has three qualifiers and a passive verb — here's how to fix it" is useful.
